# 02 — MBA Roadmap

**Canonical control:** [[Career/COO Roadmap/00_Project_Control]]

> Sourced from COO path research session, June 2026. Based on Fortune 500 C-suite data, MBA program placement analysis, and Midwest industrial recruiting patterns.

---

## The honest framing

Only ~41–46% of Fortune 500 C-suite hold MBAs at all. But of those who do in the industrial ops track, the school matters — not because prestige is magic, but because alumni network density at specific Midwest companies determines which doors open. The right question is not "do I need a top-5 MBA?" — it's "which MBA gives me the deepest network at the specific companies I'm targeting?"

For Sam's track (industrial ops → COO, Midwest focus), **Kellogg, Ross, and Booth outperform HBS and Wharton** in placement at the exact target companies. Tim Cook went to Auburn undergrad and Duke Fuqua MBA, then ran Apple's global operations and became COO. School unlocks the door — the work inside the company is what gets you to COO.

---

## Target schools

### Primary targets

**Kellogg (Northwestern)**
- Rank: Top 5 nationally; #8 in operations management
- GMAT: 700–730 median
- Why Kellogg: Evanston location = deep Midwest corporate network. Strong general management + team leadership culture maps directly to COO track. Alumni density at Caterpillar, Honeywell, Eaton, P&G, Whirlpool, 3M, Abbott. #3 nationally for general management behind HBS and Stanford.
- Watch: Skews toward consulting and general management post-MBA — make sure your ops narrative is tight.

**Michigan Ross**
- Rank: Top 15 nationally; strong ops / supply chain reputation
- GMAT: 700–720 median
- Why Ross: Best Michigan industrial alumni network in existence. BorgWarner, Ford, GM, Whirlpool, Stryker, Kellogg's, Dow all recruit here aggressively. With ISE from OU and Michigan ops credibility, this is the most natural fit and most achievable target. Strong ops and supply chain placement.
- Watch: Strong for Michigan-focused career; less global reach than Kellogg.

**Chicago Booth**
- Rank: Top 5 nationally; most analytically rigorous
- GMAT: 720–740 median
- Why Booth: Most analytically rigorous — plays directly to ISE quantitative background. Chicago placement strong into Caterpillar, Abbott, Motorola Solutions, Boeing. If PE pivot is the eventual goal (COO → Operating Partner), Booth has stronger finance/PE alumni than Kellogg or Ross. Flexible curriculum allows deep customization.
- Watch: More finance/consulting output than pure ops. Need to be intentional about ops electives.

---

### Strong fallbacks

**Ohio State Fisher**
- GMAT: 650–690 median
- Strong supply chain / ops program. Deep Ohio industrial placement (Parker Hannifin, P&G, Nationwide). Solid safety with genuine ops credibility.

**Indiana Kelley**
- GMAT: 650–690 median
- Nationally ranked #1–3 in supply chain management consistently. Very strong Midwest industrials placement. Achievable for Sam's profile. If Kellogg/Ross/Booth don't land, Kelley still opens real doors.

**MSU Broad**
- GMAT: 620–660 median
- Surprisingly strong supply chain program. Good Michigan industrial network. True safety but not a throwaway — Dow, Whirlpool, Stryker, Lear, BorgWarner all recruit here.

## What makes a strong application for ops track

**What Kellogg/Ross/Booth are looking for from ops candidates:**
- Demonstrated P&L ownership or significant financial impact (the $500K+ and $5.4M capacity projects are exactly this)
- People leadership at scale — not just project leadership
- Clear "why MBA, why now, why this school" narrative tied to specific companies/industries
- GMAT 720+ shows quantitative rigor without needing undergrad GPA to carry the whole load
- 3–5 years of post-grad experience with increasing scope

**Sam's competitive strengths for MBA apps:**
- Two verified, quantified operational impacts at the intern level (rare)
- ISE engineering background (strongest undergrad for ops MBA narrative)
- Lean Six Sigma credentials (differentiates from finance/consulting applicants)
- Rotational Fortune 500 experience (target for Summer 2027)

**Sam's gaps to close before applying:**
- People leadership at scale (formal management of a team, not just project cross-functional leadership)
- Financial literacy and P&L exposure (take corp finance course; seek budget ownership in early career)
- Global or multi-site experience (target international assignment or multi-site project by age 27–28)

## MBA vs. no MBA — the honest decision

For Sam's specific path (industrial ops → COO), the MBA is worth doing **if and only if** it's a top-10 program. A mid-tier MBA (outside top-20) does not provide enough network lift to justify the time and cost. If GMAT score doesn't reach 680+ by the second attempt, the better path is:

1. Get Black Belt + CPIM
2. Build track record as plant manager / site director
3. Let the results speak — 79% of COOs are internal appointments anyway

The MBA is a network and credibility accelerator, not a prerequisite. Many industrial COOs at companies like Eaton, Parker, and Whirlpool climbed without MBAs. But for Sam's wealth-first goal (PE partner / carried interest), the MBA network makes the eventual pivot significantly easier.
